Interview

Actual Interview Questions

The following are Interview questions I received from a company in June whose tech stack included C#/.NET/SQL Server.
"On a scale 1 to 5, how confident are you in C#?"
1. What does the static keyword mean?
2. What is the difference between an abstract and an interface?
3. What is the Linq framework in C#?
4. What is the Entity framework in .NET?
5. What does the IDisposable interface do?
6. What does SOLID state for in OOP?
7. Can you explain INNER JOINS vs OUTER JOINS?
8. What is meant by readonly?
9. What is a value reference?

These questions were simple knowledge checks pertaining to C# and OOP design principles. Coming from a Java background, I was not familiar with Linq and Entity frameworks which hurt my chances at this interview. It was actually this interview that inspired the creation of this site! I wanted there to exist a site that showed how approach the job hunt puzzle in an approachable way.

First Interview Info

There are numberous things you can be asked in a developer interview. It is important to evaluate not only whether you know the answer to these questions, but also how articulate you are when you answer them.

Keep the interviewer in mind when you are answering. This allows you to gage what the interviewer is hoping to ascertain by asking the questions. Usually during the techinal interview, the first few questions are simple knowledge checks. Things such as: "what is an array", "what is unit testing", "what is a JOIN in SQL", are all surface level questions if you have any experience with software questions.

Other interviews are code reviews, meaning writing out code to solve a problem. The point of these reviews are not actually to see if you can solve the problem, even though that would be bonus, it is an opportunity for the interviewer to see how you problem solve.

If you don't communicate and magically come up with the answer, it tells the interviewer next to nothing. Explaining how you came to solution or how you would research the code to solve it provides much information.

Studying for the Interview

The final thing to touch on is studying for the interview. You should know what market the business is in and who their potential clients are. Both of which can be found out on their website, usually in an About Us section.

As for the techinal interview, your primary study should be based on the list of job requirements/ skills that were on the application. There may technologies listed there that you are not familiar with. Research those so you are at least on speaking terms to what the technology does and how it benefits that teams' tech stack. Your more indepth study/review should be on technologies that are relevant day to day if you land the job there.

No one has the infinite time to study the 20+ techs that are listed in every job application. These job applications are usually written by hiring departments and/or senior staff who may not know the priority of the technologies listed. This means it is in your best interest to figure out what they are looking for.

The application may say front-end developer, but if the application states the applicant must have working knowledge of microservices and MongoDB then it is really a full-stack position. The company are likely building web applications that require back-end support so reviewing SQL and databases may give you an edge. Even if you are wrong this assumption, you can ask the interviewer about their current project and tech stack. This is a great opportunity to show your interest in the company and willingness to do research.
